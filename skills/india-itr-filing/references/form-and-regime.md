# Form and regime decisions

Verify all limits and due dates for the selected assessment year using the official portal before applying this guide.

## Choose the return form

- Use ITR-1 only when every eligibility condition is satisfied. Do not use it for business/professional income, capital-gain cases outside its scope, or foreign assets.
- Use ITR-2 for an eligible individual or HUF without income from business or profession when the return needs schedules such as capital gains or foreign assets.
- Use ITR-3 when the taxpayer has income from business or profession, including professional receipts, intraday trading, or F&O business activity. Salary and capital gains can coexist in ITR-3.
- Use ITR-4 only when all of its eligibility conditions are met. It is a simplified presumptive return and is not a substitute for ITR-3 when foreign assets, excluded capital gains, or other disqualifying facts exist.

Never choose a simpler form merely because the amount is small.

## Decide the tax regime

The new regime is the default. For a taxpayer with business or professional income, Form 10-IEA governs opting out of or re-entering the default regime, subject to the current rules and filing deadline.

Before choosing:

1. Compute both regimes using the same complete income data.
2. In the old regime, include only deductions and exemptions supported by records.
3. In the new regime, remove disallowed claims instead of leaving zero-value schedules selected.
4. Check the taxpayer's earlier Form 10-IEA history because business/profession cases have switching restrictions.
5. Preserve the Form 10-IEA acknowledgement when one is filed.

Do not file Form 10-IEA merely to remain in the default new regime.

## Section 44ADA

Use section 44ADA only if the taxpayer, profession, residency, entity type, and gross-receipt threshold meet the current law. Technical consultancy is among the specified professions, but describe the actual work accurately.

When using 44ADA:

- report gross professional receipts, not net bank credits after fees;
- split prescribed electronic receipts, cash, and other modes as the form requires;
- declare at least the statutory presumptive percentage unless a higher amount was actually earned and is being offered;
- do not claim the same professional expenses again because the presumptive income is after deemed expenses;
- keep invoices, remittance evidence, and the receipt reconciliation even when formal books are not required.

Do not mix unrelated trading turnover into professional receipts.

## Rent, home loan, and deductions

- HRA exemption and most Chapter VI-A deductions such as sections 80C and 80D are generally unavailable in the new regime. Verify the current exceptions.
- Rent paid is not automatically deductible. It may matter through HRA or section 80GG only when the applicable conditions and regime allow it.
- Interest on a self-occupied house property is generally not deductible in the new regime. Property under construction and pre-construction interest have separate timing rules; do not claim them before the law permits.
- Principal repayment under section 80C is an old-regime item subject to conditions and the overall limit.
- TDS deposited by a property buyer for the seller is not the buyer's personal income-tax credit.
- Health-insurance premium under section 80D is ordinarily an old-regime deduction and remains subject to eligible-person and payment-mode limits.

Always compare regimes numerically rather than assuming a large deduction makes the old regime better.
